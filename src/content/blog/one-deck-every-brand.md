Ask a communications team what happens to their brand identity after it leaves the style guide, and you'll hear the same story everywhere. The guide says Bricolage for headings; the deck from the finance department says Calibri. The guide specifies a color palette; the deck from the regional office invented three new ones.

This isn't a discipline problem. It's a tooling problem. Traditional presentation software gives every user a blank canvas and infinite freedom, then asks the brand to survive it.

## Brand as configuration

Deckyard flips this. A theme in Deckyard is a complete, centrally managed definition of how your organization looks:

- Typography: which fonts, which weights, which scale.
- Color: palette, contrast pairings, chart colors.
- Assets: logos, watermarks, image treatments.
- Layout: how each slide type composes itself.

Editors don't style slides; they fill in structured slide types. A chart slide knows what your chart colors are. A title slide knows where your logo goes. When the brand refreshes, you update the theme once and every existing deck follows.

The hero of our homepage shows the idea in one image: the same slide layout cycling through three different organizations. Same structure, same content, three completely different identities; none of them off brand, because off brand isn't a thing an editor can do.

## Freedom where it matters

The point is not to turn presenters into form-fillers. Within the rails there is plenty of room: layouts to choose, media, interactions, live polls, speaker notes. The constraint sits exactly where organizations want it (identity) and nowhere else (content).

For the organizations we're building for - nonprofits, public institutions, cultural organizations - this is often the difference between a brand identity that exists on paper and one that exists in practice. Design capacity is scarce; a theme system is the design department scaled to everyone.
