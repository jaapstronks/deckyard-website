Walk into any nonprofit, municipality or museum and you will find the same thing: an organization that runs on presentations. Board updates, funding proposals, public consultations, donor reports. Slides are how institutions think out loud.

And yet almost nobody owns that layer of their work. The decks live in a US cloud. The design lives in a template that broke the third time someone edited it. The audience data from that interactive poll tool lives somewhere you'd rather not explain to your privacy officer.

## Two problems, one product

Deckyard starts from two observations we kept running into with European organizations.

**The sovereignty problem.** Public institutions, nonprofits and cultural organizations are held to a higher standard, and increasingly hold themselves to one: GDPR, procurement rules, and a growing discomfort with routing everyday work through American hyperscalers. Presentation tools are a blind spot here. They handle strategy documents and audience data, and they are almost always US SaaS.

**The brand problem.** Most organizations have a brand identity and no way to enforce it. Every deck starts as a copy of a copy. Fonts drift, colors drift, logos stretch. The communications team fights a war of attrition against PowerPoint, and loses.

Deckyard is our answer to both at once:

- Open source (MIT), so you can read it, fork it, and never be locked in.
- Self-hostable, or hosted for you on European infrastructure.
- A theme system that treats your brand identity as configuration, not convention. Fonts, colors, logos and slide layouts are defined once, centrally; every deck anyone makes is compliant by default.
- Structured slide types (charts, timelines, polls, cards) instead of freeform canvases, so decks stay easy to create, share and publish.

> Your decks, your brand, your infrastructure. That's the whole idea.
