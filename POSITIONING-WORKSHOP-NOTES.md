*Session date: January 2026*

## Executive Summary

Deckyard's launch positioning should **lead with EU sovereignty/ownership** (the foot in the door) and **close with the innovative architecture** (structured slides + AI-ready + interoperability). These two angles reinforce each other: the open, user-controlled architecture is both a sovereignty win AND what enables the product to be genuinely better.

**The one-liner:**
> "A presentation platform that's actually yours."

---

## Audience Definition

### Primary audience (launch)
Developers and technical decision-makers in Europe (and sovereignty-conscious folks elsewhere) who are actively seeking non-US alternatives for their tooling. They care about:
- Data sovereignty and GDPR compliance
- Self-hosting capability
- Open source / MIT license
- Avoiding US SaaS lock-in

### Secondary audience (discovered later)
The broader "people who present" market—marketing teams, trainers, educators, nonprofits—who discover Deckyard through the primary audience or sovereignty-focused press, then stay because the product is genuinely good.

### The journey
> "Came for the sovereignty. Stayed for the product."

## Positioning Framework

### Lead positioning (door opener)
**EU sovereignty + open source + self-hostable**

This is credible, differentiated, and timely:
- Netherlands company, French hosting (Scaleway), no US in the chain
- MIT licensed, fully open source
- Docker self-hosting in minutes
- Political momentum for EU digital autonomy ("eurostack")

### Supporting positioning (why they stay)
**Opinionated templates + agentic-ready architecture**

The deeper value proposition:
- 44 structured slide types that encode design decisions
- Users fill in content, not design layouts
- Because slides are structured, AI can actually work with them reliably
- Full API, MCP-ready, open formats (Markdown in, JSON storage, multiple exports)
- Interoperability by design

### The architectural insight
> "Presentations should work like websites. Design creates templates. Editors fill in content. The system enforces the constraints."

When you add AI to an *opinionated* system, the AI can reliably produce on-brand output because the constraints make "correct" the only option.

## SWOT Analysis

### Strengths
- Genuine EU sovereignty story (Netherlands company, French hosting, no US in chain)
- Full-featured platform (collaboration, publishing, embedding, interactions, translations)
- Opinionated design system (44 slide types encoding best practices)
- AI/agentic architecture (document-to-slides, API, MCP coming)
- Open source / self-hostable (MIT license, Docker deployment)
- Solo founder agility (fast decisions, no committee)

### Weaknesses
- Unknown brand (no awareness, no social proof yet)
- Solo founder resource constraints (support, marketing, dev all compete)
- "Restrictive" positioning risk (template approach may feel limiting to some)
- No existing user base (chicken-and-egg for social proof)

### Opportunities
- EU digital sovereignty momentum (political/regulatory tailwinds)
- Backlash against US SaaS (pricing, data practices, enshittification)
- AI-native tools category emerging (early, not late)
- Open source community (contributors, advocates)
- Underserved niches (government, education, nonprofits with compliance needs)

### Threats
- Established players adding features (Google Slides adds polls, etc.)
- AI noise (every tool claims AI, hard to stand out)
- Open source sustainability (funding continued development)
- Enterprise sales cycles (sovereignty buyers often have slow procurement)

## Credibility Strategy (No Social Proof Yet)

Since there are no customers yet, credibility comes from:

1. **Open source launch buzz** — Launching something big, bold, and MIT licensed generates attention
2. **Personal credibility** — Founder's background and reputation in the space
3. **Seed users** — Founder's company uses it; parent organization interested
4. **Sandbox experience** — "Just try it, see for yourself" (self-destructing demo)
5. **EU sovereignty press** — Digital autonomy / eurostack circles will amplify
6. **Developer UX** — Easy "deploy in 3 steps" experience builds word of mouth
7. **Creative stunts** — e.g., "First 5 people to self-host and share a link get a chocolate cake delivered"

## Key Section Drafts

### Section 2: "Built for how we work now"

**Headline:**
> Built for how we work now

**Lead:**
> Presentations shouldn't be design projects. They should be structured content—ideas organized into patterns that just work. Deckyard gives you 44 opinionated slide types. You focus on your message. The design is already handled.

**Three pillars:**

**Structured slides, not blank canvases**
> Every slide type encodes design decisions. KPIs, timelines, charts, matrices—pick the right one, fill in your content, done. No wrestling with layouts. No inconsistent decks.

**AI that understands your content**
> Because slides are structured, AI can actually work with them. Generate a deck from a document. Update quarterly numbers automatically. The constraints enable the automation.

**Open formats, interoperable by design**
> Markdown in, JSON storage, PDF and PowerPoint out. Full API. MCP-ready. Your presentations aren't trapped—they're part of your workflow.

---

### Section 5: AI (65/35 positive/defensive balance)

**Headline:**
> AI that actually works—on your terms

**Lead:**
> Generate entire presentations from a prompt. Convert documents into structured decks. Translate slides into any language. Deckyard's AI works reliably because the slide system was designed for it.

**Key points:**

**Bring your own keys**
> Connect OpenAI, Anthropic, Mistral, DeepSeek, or run local models. Pay your provider directly at their rates—no credits, no markup, no limits.

**Structured slides = reliable output**
> AI tools struggle with freeform canvases. Deckyard's opinionated slide types give AI clear targets. The result: consistent, usable decks—not random layouts.

**You're in control**
> Choose your provider. Self-host to keep prompts on your infrastructure. Or disable AI entirely—Deckyard works great without it.

## Key Strategic Decisions Made

1. **Lead with sovereignty, close with product** — EU/open source is the door opener; innovative architecture is why they stay

2. **Primary CTA is sandbox** — Lowest friction proof point; people will assume self-hosting is easy if you say it is

3. **.eu domain is intentional** — Willing to be seen as political, Europe all the way

4. **"Restrictive templates" needs positive framing** — Not "you can't mess it up" but "focus on your message, we've handled the design"

5. **AI framing is "on your terms"** — Appeals to power users (choice!) and skeptics (control!) simultaneously

6. **Open source is a trust/proof mechanism** — Not expecting contributor community, but important for credibility

7. **Competitive framing is implied, not explicit** — Lead with Deckyard's strengths, contrast is secondary
